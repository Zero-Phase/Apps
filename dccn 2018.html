(d) Question: What are transmission impairments? Briefly explain each. [5]

Answer:
 Transmission impairments are any undesirable effects that degrade the quality of a signal as it travels through a transmission medium from a sender to a receiver. When a signal is transmitted, it is not received in its original form due to these impairments. The three primary types of transmission impairments are:

  1. Attenuation: Attenuation refers to the loss of signal strength or energy as it propagates through the transmission medium. This loss occurs because the medium absorbs some of the signal's energy, converting it into heat. For a signal to be successfully received and interpreted, it must have sufficient strength. To compensate for this loss, amplifiers (for analog signals) or repeaters (for digital signals) are used to boost the signal strength at regular intervals.
  2. Distortion: Distortion refers to the change in the shape or form of the signal. This type of impairment is caused by the fact that different frequency components of a signal travel at different speeds through the medium. Since a composite signal is made up of various frequencies, some components may arrive at the receiver at different times than others, leading to a phase shift and a change in the overall signal shape.
  3. Noise:Noise is any unwanted random signal that is added to and interferes with the original transmitted signal. It is a major limiting factor in communication system performance. There are several types of noise, including:
    • Thermal Noise: Caused by the random thermal motion of electrons in a conductor. It is unavoidable and present in all electronic devices and media.
    • Crosstalk: Interference from an adjacent signal path or channel. For example, in twisted-pair cabling, signals from one pair of wires can interfere with signals in another pair.
    • Impulse Noise: Irregular pulses or noise spikes of short duration and high amplitude, often caused by external electromagnetic disturbances such as lightning or power line surges.



(e) Question: Briefly explain the Pulse Code Modulation Technique (PCM). [5]

Answer:
 Pulse Code Modulation (PCM) is the standard method used to convert an analog signal into a digital signal so that it can be transmitted through a digital communication network. The PCM process involves three main steps:

  1. Sampling: The first step is to sample the continuous-time analog signal at discrete, regular intervals. This process converts the continuous signal into a series of discrete-time pulses, where each pulse's amplitude is equal to the analog signal's amplitude at that specific instant. According to the Nyquist theorem, the sampling rate must be at least twice the highest frequency component of the original analog signal (fₛ ≥ 2fₘₐₓ) to ensure the original signal can be reconstructed without loss of information.
  2. Quantization: The result of sampling is a series of pulses with varying amplitudes. Quantization is the process of mapping these infinite possible amplitude values to a finite set of predefined discrete levels. Each sample's amplitude is approximated to the nearest quantization level. This step inevitably introduces an error, known as quantization error, which is the difference between the actual sample amplitude and its mapped quantization value.
  3. Encoding: In the final step, each quantized level is assigned a unique binary code word. For example, if there are 256 (2⁸) quantization levels, each level is represented by an 8-bit binary number. These binary code words are then concatenated to form the final digital signal, which is a stream of bits ready for transmission. This binary stream is what is transmitted over the digital network.

  • Reduces electromagnetic interference (EMI) by canceling out magnetic fields generated by the wires.

(d) Question: Briefly explain the working mechanism of Frequency-Division Multiplexing Technique (FDM). [5]

Answer:
 Frequency-Division Multiplexing (FDM) is an analog multiplexing technique that combines multiple signals for transmission over a single medium by assigning each signal a unique, non-overlapping frequency band.

The working mechanism involves the following steps:

  1. Channel Allocation: The total bandwidth of the transmission medium is divided into several smaller, separate frequency bands, with each band serving as a channel.
  2. Modulation: Each of the individual low-bandwidth signals to be transmitted (called baseband signals) is modulated onto a different, high-frequency carrier signal. This process shifts the baseband signal into its allocated frequency channel. For example, signal 1 is modulated onto carrier f₁, signal 2 onto carrier f₂, and so on.
  3. Combining: The modulated signals, now occupying different frequency bands, are combined into a single, composite high-bandwidth signal by a multiplexer (MUX).
  4. Guard Bands: To prevent interference and signal overlap between adjacent channels, small, unused frequency bands known as guard bands are inserted between the channels.
  5. Transmission: The composite signal is transmitted over the single high-capacity link.
  6. Demultiplexing: At the receiving end, a demultiplexer (DEMUX) uses a series of filters to separate the composite signal back into its original component channels. Each filtered signal is then demodulated to recover the original baseband signal.



(e) Question: Write advantages and disadvantages of Optical Fibre Cable. [5]

Answer:
 Optical fiber cable is a high-speed transmission medium that transmits data as pulses of light. It has several distinct advantages and disadvantages.

Advantages of Optical Fibre Cable:

  • Higher Bandwidth: Fiber optic cables can support significantly higher bandwidth and data rates than copper cables, allowing for the transmission of more information.
  • Less Signal Attenuation: Signals travel longer distances through fiber with less signal loss (attenuation) compared to copper, reducing the need for signal boosters or repeaters.
  • Immunity to Electromagnetic Interference (EMI): Since fiber optic cables transmit data using light instead of electricity, they are completely immune to EMI, crosstalk, and impulse noise from power lines, motors, or other electrical sources.
  • Enhanced Security: It is very difficult to tap into a fiber optic cable without being detected, as tapping requires disrupting the light signal, which is easily noticeable. This provides a higher level of security compared to copper wires.
  • Smaller Size and Lighter Weight: An optical fiber cable is much thinner and lighter than a copper cable with the same information-carrying capacity, making it easier to install, especially in crowded conduits.
  • Greater Durability: Fiber optic cable is more resistant to corrosion and harsh environmental conditions compared to copper cable.

Disadvantages of Optical Fibre Cable:

  • High Installation Cost: The cost of the cable itself, as well as the associated installation, specialized tools, and network interface components, is generally higher than for copper-based systems.
  • Fragility: The glass or plastic fibers are more fragile and susceptible to physical damage from bending or breaking than copper wires. They must be handled and installed with care.
  • Complexity of Installation and Maintenance: Installing, splicing, and terminating fiber optic cables requires specialized skills and equipment. Repairing a broken fiber is also a more complex process than repairing a copper wire.
  • Unidirectional Light Propagation: Light can only travel in one direction down a fiber. For two-way communication, two separate fibers are required, one for transmitting and one for receiving.

Answer:
 The two approaches to packet switching are:

  1. Datagram Network Approach (Connectionless): In this approach, each packet is treated as an independent unit, called a datagram.   2. (b) Question: Distinguish between VRC and LRC. What is a parity bit? [2+1=3]

Answer:
Parity Bit:
 A parity bit is a single bit that is appended to a block of data, such as a byte or character, for the purpose of error detection. The value of the parity bit is set so that the total number of '1's in the data block (including the parity bit) is either always even (even parity) or always odd (odd parity). The receiver checks the parity; if it does not match the agreed-upon scheme (even or odd), an error is detected.

Distinction between VRC and LRC:

  • Vertical Redundancy Check (VRC):
    • Also known as a simple parity check.
    • In VRC, one parity bit is appended to each data unit (e.g., each byte or character).
    • It is "vertical" because if the data bits are arranged in a table, the VRC parity bit is calculated for each row.
    • VRC can detect all single-bit errors in a data unit. However, it cannot detect an even number of bit errors (e.g., two, four) within the same data unit, as these errors would cancel each other out and result in a "correct" parity.
  • Longitudinal Redundancy Check (LRC):
    • In LRC, a block of data units is organized into a table (rows and columns), and a parity bit is calculated for each column.
    • The collection of these column-wise parity bits forms a new data unit called the Block Check Character (BCC), which is appended to the entire block of data.
    • LRC is more robust than VRC. When used in combination with VRC (a two-dimensional parity check), it can detect all single-bit and double-bit errors, and most multi-bit burst errors. It can even correct single-bit errors by identifying the row and column where the error occurred.



(c) Question: What is ALOHA? [1]

Answer:
 ALOHA is a pioneering medium access control (MAC) protocol for shared-medium networks. It is a random access protocol, meaning that multiple stations can transmit data over a shared channel at will. The basic principle of ALOHA is that a station transmits a frame whenever it has data to send. If the frame collides with a transmission from another station, the corrupted frames are discarded. The sending stations, after detecting the collision (usually via a lack of acknowledgment), wait for a random amount of time before retransmitting the frame. This random backoff period is crucial to reduce the probability of repeated collisions. There are two main versions: Pure ALOHA (transmits anytime) and Slotted ALOHA (transmits only at the beginning of a time slot), with the latter having better channel utilization.



(d) Question: Briefly explain the working principle of Cyclic Redundancy Check (CRC). How does a dataword differ from a codeword? [4+1=5]

Answer:
Working Principle of Cyclic Redundancy Check (CRC):
 Cyclic Redundancy Check (CRC) is a powerful error-detecting code commonly used in digital networks. Its working principle is based on binary polynomial division.

  1. Agreement on a Generator Polynomial: Both the sender and receiver must agree on a predetermined binary number, known as the generator polynomial (or divisor), G.
  2. Sender's Side (Generation of Checksum):
    • The sender appends a string of n zeros to the end of the original dataword (the message), where n is one less than the number of bits in the generator G.
    • This new, longer bit string is then divided by the generator G using binary division (modulo-2 arithmetic).
    • The remainder resulting from this division is the CRC checksum, which will have n bits.
    • The sender replaces the appended zeros with this CRC checksum, forming the final codeword. This codeword is then transmitted.
  3. Receiver's Side (Error Checking):
    • The receiver gets the transmitted codeword (original data + CRC checksum).
    • It divides the entire received codeword by the same generator polynomial G.
    • If the remainder of this division is zero, the receiver assumes the data is error-free and accepts it.
    • If the remainder is non-zero, an error is detected, and the receiver discards the data, typically requesting a retransmission.

Difference between Dataword and Codeword:

  • Dataword: The dataword is the original block of data bits that needs to be transmitted. It is the raw information from the upper layers. Let's say it has k bits.
  • Codeword: The codeword is what is actually transmitted over the link. It is formed by taking the original dataword and appending redundant bits (the error-checking bits, such as the CRC checksum). If r redundant bits are added to a k-bit dataword, the resulting codeword will have a length of k + r bits. The codeword contains both the original data and the means to check its integrity.



(e) Question: Explain each phase of a Virtual Circuit Network. [5]

Answer:
 A Virtual Circuit Network is a connection-oriented packet-switching approach. Communication in such a network occurs in three distinct phases:

  1. Setup Phase (Connection Establishment):
    • Before any data can be transferred, a logical connection or path, known as a virtual circuit (VC), must be established between the source and destination nodes.
    • The source sends a special "setup request" or "call request" packet to the destination. This packet is routed through the network, and as it travels, each router along the path makes an entry in its routing table for this specific connection. The entry includes the source and destination ports and assigns a Virtual Circuit Identifier (VCI) for the connection on that link.
    • If the destination is able to accept the connection, it sends back a "call accepted" or "acknowledgment" packet along the same path. Once the source receives this acknowledgment, the virtual circuit is established and ready for data transfer.
  2. Data Transfer Phase:
    • Once the virtual circuit is established, all data packets belonging to the message are transmitted along this pre-determined path.
    • The packets do not need to contain the full source and destination addresses. Instead, each packet carries the small, locally significant VCI. When a router receives a packet, it looks up the VCI in its table to determine the outgoing port and the new VCI to use for the next hop.
    • Because all packets follow the same path, they typically arrive at the destination in the correct order.
  3. Teardown Phase (Connection Termination):
    • When the sender has no more data to transmit, it initiates the termination of the virtual circuit.
    • The source sends a "teardown request" or "clear request" packet to the destination along the established path.
    • As this packet propagates, each router along the path removes the corresponding entry from its table, releasing the resources (VCI, buffer space) that were allocated to that virtual circuit.
    • The destination may confirm the termination with a "clear confirmation" packet. After this phase, the virtual circuit no longer exists.

(d) Question: What is the Nyquist Theorem? [2]

Answer:
The Nyquist Theorem, in the context of data communications, specifies the maximum theoretical data rate (channel capacity) for a noiseless channel. It states that the maximum bit rate is directly proportional to the bandwidth of the channel and the number of signal levels used.

The formula is:
C = 2 × B × log₂(L)

Where:

  • C is the maximum channel capacity in bits per second (bps).
  • B is the bandwidth of the channel in Hertz (Hz).
  • L is the number of discrete signal levels used to represent data.

The theorem implies that for a given bandwidth, the data rate can be increased by increasing the number of signal levels. However, in practice, increasing the number of levels makes the system more susceptible to noise.

(f) Question: What is quantization error in PCM? [2]

Answer:
 Quantization error, also known as quantization noise, is the error introduced during the quantization step of the Pulse Code Modulation (PCM) process. It is defined as the difference between the actual amplitude of the analog signal sample and the discrete quantization level to which it is mapped. Since the original analog signal can have an infinite number of amplitude values, while the quantization process uses a finite number of discrete levels, this approximation is necessary and inevitably results in a loss of information, which manifests as this error or noise in the reconstructed signal.



(g) Question: Define UDP. [2]

Answer:
UDP, or User Datagram Protocol, is one of the core protocols of the Transport Layer in the TCP/IP protocol suite. It is a connectionless and unreliable protocol. UDP provides a simple mechanism for sending datagrams from one application to another with minimal protocol overhead. "Connectionless" means no prior connection needs to be established before sending data. "Unreliable" means UDP does not guarantee delivery, order of arrival, or integrity of the data. It is often used for applications where speed and low overhead are more critical than reliability, such as in real-time video/audio streaming, online gaming, and DNS (Domain Name System) queries.

(b) Question: What is a mask in IPv4 addressing? What is a default mask in IPv4 addressing? [1+1=2]

Answer:
Mask in IPv4 Addressing:
 A mask, also known as a subnet mask or netmask, is a 32-bit number used in IPv4 addressing to divide an IP address into two parts: the network address and the host address. The mask works by using a series of consecutive '1's followed by a series of consecutive '0's. The bits in the IP address that correspond to the '1's in the mask represent the network portion, while the bits that correspond to the '0's represent the host portion. This mechanism is fundamental to IP routing, allowing routers to determine if a destination IP address is on the local network or a remote network.

Default Mask in IPv4 Addressing:
 A default mask is a pre-defined subnet mask that was used in the original classful addressing scheme to identify the network and host portions for each address class (A, B, and C). The default masks are:

  • Class A: 255.0.0.0 (or /8 in CIDR notation)
  • Class B: 255.255.0.0 (or /16 in CIDR notation)
  • Class C: 255.255.255.0 (or /24 in CIDR notation)



(c) Question: Write the functions of the Transport layer and Network layer of the ISO/OSI model. [2+2=4]

Answer:
Functions of the Network Layer (Layer 3):
 The Network Layer is responsible for the delivery of individual packets from the source host to the destination host across multiple networks. Its primary functions include:

  • Logical Addressing: It provides a unique logical address (e.g., an IP address) for every device on the network, distinguishing it from physical (MAC) addressing.
  • Routing: It determines the best path for data packets to travel from source to destination. This is achieved using routing protocols to build and maintain routing tables.
  • Packet Forwarding: It forwards packets from an incoming link to the appropriate outgoing link based on the routing table and the packet's destination address.
  • Fragmentation: If a packet is larger than the maximum size supported by a downstream network (MTU), the network layer fragments it into smaller packets.

Functions of the Transport Layer (Layer 4):
 The Transport Layer is responsible for providing reliable or unreliable process-to-process message delivery and error recovery. Its key functions include:

  • Process-to-Process Delivery: While the network layer delivers packets from host to host, the transport layer delivers data segments to the correct application process on the host, using port numbers for addressing.
  • Segmentation and Reassembly: It breaks up large messages from the session layer into smaller, manageable units called segments. At the destination, it reassembles these segments back into the original message.
  • Connection Control: It can be connection-oriented (like TCP), establishing a logical connection before data transfer, or connectionless (like UDP).
  • Flow Control: It manages the data transmission rate between two hosts to prevent a fast sender from overwhelming a slow receiver.
  • Error Control: It provides end-to-end error checking and recovery. This includes using checksums to detect corrupted segments and sequence numbers to identify lost or duplicate segments, triggering retransmission when necessary.

(e) Question: Write the working mechanism of a Distance-Vector Routing Protocol. [4]

Answer:
 A Distance-Vector Routing Protocol is a class of routing algorithms where routers determine the best path to a destination based on a metric of "distance" (such as hop count, delay, or cost). The working mechanism is often summarized as "routing by rumor" because each router relies on the information provided by its immediate neighbors.

The mechanism operates as follows:

  1. Initialization: Each router starts with a routing table that contains entries only for its directly connected networks. The distance to these networks is typically set to 0 or 1. The distance to all other networks is initialized to infinity.
  2. Information Sharing: At regular, periodic intervals (e.g., every 30 seconds), each router broadcasts its entire routing table to all of its directly connected neighbors. This table contains a list of all networks the router knows about and its calculated distance (cost) to each of them.
  3. Route Calculation and Table Update:
    • When a router receives a routing table from a neighbor, it examines the information.
    • For each destination in the neighbor's table, the router calculates its own distance to that destination by adding the cost of reaching the neighbor to the distance the neighbor reported for that destination.
    • The router then compares this newly calculated distance with the distance it currently has in its own routing table for that same destination.
    • If the new path via the neighbor is shorter (has a lower cost), the router updates its routing table with the new, better route, listing the neighbor as the next hop.
  4. Convergence: This process of sharing and updating repeats. Over time, the routing information propagates throughout the network, and all routers eventually learn the best paths to all destinations. The network is said to have "converged" when all routers have consistent and stable routing tables.

A key algorithm underlying this process is the Bellman-Ford algorithm. A well-known example of a distance-vector protocol is the Routing Information Protocol (RIP).



(f) Question: Explain the working mechanism of a Link-State Routing Protocol. [4]

Answer:
 A Link-State Routing Protocol is a class of routing algorithms where each router constructs a complete topological map of the entire network. This allows each router to make an independent routing decision based on this full understanding of the network's layout. The working mechanism is more complex than distance-vector but converges faster and is less prone to routing loops.

The mechanism involves five key steps:

  1. Discover Neighbors: When a router is initialized, it first learns about its directly connected neighbors. It sends a special "Hello" packet out on each of its links. Routers that receive this packet reply, allowing the initial router to build a list of its adjacent neighbors.
  2. Measure Link Cost: The router then determines the cost (or metric) of the link to each of its neighbors. This cost can be based on factors like bandwidth, delay, or a simple hop count. This can be done by sending echo packets and measuring the round-trip time.
  3. Build a Link-State Packet (LSP): Each router compiles the information it has gathered—its identity, the list of its neighbors, and the cost to each neighbor—into a small packet called a Link-State Packet (LSP) or Link-State Advertisement (LSA).
  4. Flood the LSP to All Routers: The router then floods this LSP to all other routers in the network. Each router that receives an LSP makes a copy and forwards the LSP to all its neighbors except the one it came from. This flooding mechanism ensures that every router in the network quickly receives a copy of the LSP from every other router.
  5. Compute the Shortest Path: Once a router has collected a complete set of LSPs from all other routers, it has a full map of the network topology. It then uses this map as input to a shortest-path algorithm, typically Dijkstra's algorithm, to compute the shortest path from itself to every other destination in the network. The results of this calculation are used to build the final routing table.
